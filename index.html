Methodology & Data Notes

ETF characteristics are approximate values sourced from Vanguard and Avantis fund data as of . All metrics are estimates and should not be used as the sole basis for investment decisions.

  • P/E Ratio: Blended using the harmonic mean (weighted average of earnings yields), which prevents distortion from high-P/E outliers and correctly reflects the aggregate earnings yield of the portfolio.
  • Earnings Yield: 1/PE × 100%. A commonly used simplified proxy for expected real returns over the long run.
  • Value Tilt (HML): Fama-French High-Minus-Low factor loading. 0 ≈ market-cap blend; higher = more tilted toward value stocks.
  • Size Tilt (SMB): Fama-French Small-Minus-Big factor loading. 0 ≈ large-cap weighted; higher = more tilted toward small-cap stocks.
  • Factor Model Return Estimate (equities): = RF (4.0%) + CAPE-adjusted ERP + HML × 3.5% (value premium) + SMB × 2.5% (size premium). The equity risk premium is derived from current valuations: ERP = 100/CAPE + 4.5% long-run nominal EPS growth − RF. US equities use Shiller CAPE ≈ 37 (ERP ≈ 3.2%); international developed markets use CAPE ≈ 16 (ERP ≈ 6.75%). The ERP is blended by each portfolio's US vs. international equity weight. Factor premiums are not guaranteed to persist in the future.
  • Fixed Income Return Estimate (BND): Uses the current SEC yield as the expected gross return proxy. Blended with the equity factor return proportionally by allocation weight.
  • Expense Ratio: Weighted average annual fund cost. Already subtracted from all return estimates shown.
  • Portfolio Volatility: Computed via the Markowitz formula — σ_p = √(ΣᵢΣⱼ wᵢwⱼσᵢσⱼρᵢⱼ) — using approximate pairwise ETF correlations. This properly captures diversification: a blended portfolio is less volatile than the sum of its parts.
  • Geometric Return & Variance Drag: The factor model produces an arithmetic mean return. Over time, volatility reduces compounded growth via variance drag: geometric ≈ arithmetic − σ²/2. The 30-year growth projection uses this geometric (CAGR) rate as the median path.
  • Monthly Contributions / Withdrawals: The ±$/mo field adds a fixed monthly cash flow to the projection. Positive values = contributions (accumulation); negative = withdrawals (decumulation). Uses proper monthly compounding: annuity FV = C × ((1+r)^y − 1) / r_monthly. In real mode, the contribution amount is treated as constant in today's dollars. The portfolio is floored at $0 (depletion).
  • Uncertainty Band (Growth Chart): The shaded region shows ±1 standard deviation of outcomes, derived from the lognormal distribution: at year t the band is median × exp(±σ√t), covering roughly 68% of expected outcomes. When monthly cash flows are non-zero, the bands are an approximation — the true distribution with contributions is more complex. Rebalancing and taxes are not modeled.
  • This tool is for educational and illustrative purposes only. Not investment advice.

After-Tax Yield by Federal Tax Bracket
Methodology & Notes

Compares the after-tax yield of tax-exempt municipal bond funds against taxable bond funds across federal income tax brackets.

  • Tax-Exempt funds (munis): Interest is exempt from federal income tax. After-tax yield equals the stated yield regardless of bracket.
  • Taxable funds: After-tax yield = stated yield × (1 − marginal rate). Higher brackets erode more of the yield.
  • Tax Equivalent Yield (TEY): The taxable yield needed to match a muni's after-tax yield. TEY = muni yield ÷ (1 − rate). Shown in gray beneath muni values.
  • Winner: The fund with the highest after-tax yield for that bracket is highlighted green.
  • State taxes: Most muni funds are also exempt from state taxes in the issuing state, making them even more attractive in high-tax states. Not modelled here.
  • Yields change frequently. Update the SEC yields above to reflect current data before drawing conclusions.

Methodology & Data Notes

Historical risk statistics for blended stock/bond portfolios, sourced from Vanguard Portfolio Allocation Models covering 1926–2024. Stocks are represented by US equity indices (S&P 500 and predecessors); bonds by US investment-grade indices (Bloomberg US Aggregate predecessors). All figures are approximate and rounded. Annual returns are nominal.

  • Annual Volatility (σ): The standard deviation of annual calendar-year returns. A portfolio with σ=10% returned within roughly ±10% of its mean in about two-thirds of years.
  • The U-shape in volatility: A key insight from portfolio theory — adding bonds to an all-stock portfolio initially decreases total volatility, because bonds and stocks are imperfectly correlated. The minimum-volatility portfolio historically sits around 20–30% equity. Adding bonds beyond that point offers only marginal further risk reduction, and an all-bond portfolio can itself be volatile (e.g., 2022: bonds lost ~13%).
  • Worst Calendar Year: The single worst annual return observed in the historical record for that equity allocation. This is a measure of short-term downside risk, not the maximum peak-to-trough drawdown. The deepest multi-year drawdowns (e.g., 1929–1932 for equities: −83% cumulative) are far larger than a single calendar year shows.
  • Best Calendar Year: The single best annual return observed. Note that bond-heavy portfolios had strong best years in 1982 (when interest rates peaked and fell sharply), which is why the best-year figure does not monotonically increase with equity allocation.
  • Maximum Years to Recovery: The longest time (in years) it took a portfolio of that allocation to return to its prior peak, measured in nominal terms over 1926–2024. The worst case for 100% stocks was the 1929 crash, which took approximately 15 years to recover nominally (by ~1944). Recovery times compound non-linearly: going from 100% to 80% stocks roughly cuts recovery time in half, and a 60/40 portfolio has historically recovered in about 6 years or less. This metric is particularly relevant for investors who may need to draw down assets within a decade.
  • Return vs. Risk trade-off: Higher equity allocations have delivered higher long-run average returns but also higher volatility and deeper periodic losses. The right allocation depends on your time horizon, ability to tolerate losses without selling, and need for the money.
  • Limitations: This data reflects US stocks and US bonds only. International diversification can alter these characteristics. Past return and risk figures do not guarantee future results. Rebalancing frequency and taxes are not modelled.
